üßÆ How Your Grade Is Calculated

All assessments in this course are standardized so that the median score is set to 50 and the MADN (Median Absolute Deviation, normalized) is set to 25.

The scale is designed to feel intuitive: values near 0 always represent very low performance, values near 100 always represent very high performance, and 50 always represents the class average.

Each standardized score is then averaged according to the weights listed in Table 3 of the syllabus to produce a course total. Because this weighting can introduce small shifts in the distribution, the totals are standardized once more. The final standardized score is then compared against the letter grade cutoffs shown in Table 2 of the syllabus.


ü§î FAQ About Your Grades

Why are you standardising?

Raw percentage scores can be misleading because the same number can reflect very different levels of performance depending on the difficulty of the assessment. For example, a score of 60% might be excellent on a challenging exam, while 85% could be only average on an easier one. In essense, basic percentage scores fail to take into account how the class performed as a whole, which makes it difficult to set meaningful grade boundaries in advance and can contribute to grade inflation, since instructors often feel pressured to award higher marks.

Standardizing scores, by contrast, evaluates your performance based on how far above or below the class average you are (i.e., you are evaluated relative to your peers). This approach ensures that a difficult assessment does not unfairly lower everyone‚Äôs grades, and that an easy one does not artificially inflate them. The standardized scale keeps grades tied to their intended meaning at the University, providing a fairer and more consistent measure of achievement. If all instructors adopted a similar system, grades would be more equitable across different sections of the same course, regardless of how easy or difficult a particular course happened to be.

Why are you using the median and MADN statistics?

The median score is a robust measure of the class average. Similarly, the MADN is a robust measure of spread. In statistics, "robust" means that the method is not thrown off by unusual values or outliers. For example, if one student happens to score extremely low or extremely high, a robust method won't let that single score shift the whole class's average or spread. These choices make the standardized scale more stable and fair, especially in classes where exams or assignments occasionally produce extreme results.

Is this the same as "grading on a curve?"

No. Grading on a curve predetermines how many students can receive each letter grade. i.e., Students are ranked and only the top \(x\)% of students get an A+, the next \(x\)% get an A, the next \(x\)% get an A- and so on. This course uses no quotas. The number of A's, B's, C's, etc., emerges from the class's actual performance, not an imposed formula ‚Äî fully in line with University policy prohibiting mandatory curves. See University of Alberta Assessment and Grading Policy

How do I convert a raw score to a standardized value? Can you show an example?

You don't need to calculate this yourself‚Äîthe gradebook does it automatically. But if you'd like to double-check, the standardized score is computed much like a z-score, except it uses the class median as the centre and the normalized median absolute deviation (MADN) as the measure of spread. The formula is:

\[ s = \left(\frac{x - m}{\text{MADN}}\right) \times 25 + 50 \]

where \(x\) is the raw score, \(m\) is the class median, and MADN measures the spread. The constants 25 and 50 set the scale so that one MADN corresponds to 25 points, and the class median corresponds to 50.

  • Suppose the class median on an assignment is \(m = 72\)
  • Suppose the \(\text{MADN} = 11.9\)
  • For a student with a raw score of \(x = 88\), we compute:
\[ \begin{align} s &= \left( \frac{88 - 72}{11.9}\right) \times 25 + 50 \\[1em] &\approx 83.6 \end{align} \] So, in this example, a raw score of 88 converts to a standardized score of about 83.6. This indicates performance well above the class average (50), and it would then be compared to the fixed letter grade boundaries in Table 2 of the syllabus.
How do I combine/average all my scores by their weights?

Your gradebook already does this for you; however, if you want to double-check, use a weighted average (a.k.a. weighted mean):

Suppose you completed two assignments and a midterm and their scores and weights are as follows:

Assessment Score Weight
Assign. 1 62 8
Assign. 2 59 10
Midterm 43 20

Just multiply each score by it's weight, add the products, and divide by the sum of the weights:

\[ \begin{align} \text{Overall} &= \frac{(8 \times 62) + (10 \times 59) + (20 \times 43)}{8 + 10 + 20} \\[1em] &= \frac{496 + 590 + 860}{38} \\[1em] & \approx 51.21 \end{align} \]


The formula looks like this:

\[ \text{Overall} \;=\; \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} w_i \, s_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{k} w_i} \]

  • \(s_i = \) score for assignment \(i\).
  • \(w_i = \) for assignment \(i\).
  • \(k = \) number of assessments.

Is this grading system competitive?

Not in the sense of fixed quotas or forced scarcity. Your grade does not depend on ‚Äúbeating‚Äù a certain number of classmates, and there is no preset limit on how many A's, B's, or C's can be awarded. That being said, strong performance is still rewarded: students who consistently perform well above the average earn higher standardized scores and thus higher letter grades.

Can everyone get an A?

Not realistically. An A or A+ is meant to signal extraordinary performance ‚Äî achievement that clearly stands out from the rest of the class. If everyone earned the same top grade, then by definition no one's performance would be extraordinary (it would be ordinary), and the grade would lose its meaning. Because scores are standardized around the class median, only those who perform well above that benchmark end up in the A range. In practice, this means that while many students can do well, top grades remain a mark of truly exceptional achievement.

If the median is always set to 50 (B-), does that mean half the class will fail?

No. The median is simply an anchor point for the scale. Students above 50 are above average and may receive grades ranging from B- to A+; students below 50 are below average but may still earn passing grades (C's and D's). In most classes, the majority of students pass. The exact distribution of grades depends on how the whole class performs, not on a fixed quota.

Does this system disadvantage strong students?

No. Because ranks are preserved, students who consistently perform well compared to their peers will always receive higher standardized scores and higher letter grades.
